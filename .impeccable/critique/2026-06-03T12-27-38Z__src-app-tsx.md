## Anti-Patterns Verdict

- **LLM Assessment**: The visual layout is highly functional but suffers from minor AI-generated slop tells: repeated card grids, bouncy button/badge animations, and prominent left-border accents that visually compete with actual content.
- **Deterministic Scan**:
  - **Overused Font**: Flagged in `src/pdf.ts` (line 17) for using Inter.
  - **Side-Tab Accent Borders**: Flagged in `src/styles.css` (lines 235, 288, 479, 643) on safety cards, metric blocks, warning cards, and instruction cards.
  - **Bounce Easing**: Flagged in `src/styles.css` (lines 420, 467) for using bouncy cubic-bezier timing functions on pill and hold-pill hover states.
- **Visual Overlays**: No visual overlay runs since we are performing a file-level scan.

## Overall Impression
WarfarinPro is a highly performant and keyboard-efficient tool for clinical settings. However, it relies on several visual tells that make it look "AI-generated" (springy pill hover curves and left-accent borders). Refining these visual cues will elevate it into a professional-grade medical application.

## What's Working
- **Clinical Efficiency**: Alt-shortcuts and Enter-based tab transitions enable superfast, hands-on-keyboard calculations for busy clinicians.
- **Immediate Value Feedback**: Adjusting calculations immediately updates the maintenance schedule, calculated weekly dose, and quality metrics without lagging.

## Priority Issues

### [P1] AI Slop: Side-Tab Accent Borders
- **Why it matters**: Alerts, warnings, and metric components use a `4px` or `5px` colored left-border. This is a classic AI-slop visual pattern that adds visual noise and distracts from high-stakes medical copy.
- **Fix**: Replace side-tab borders with solid borders, soft background card fills, and status-specific icons.
- **Suggested command**: `/impeccable quieter`

### [P1] Unstable Motion: Springy Hover Easing on Pills
- **Why it matters**: Hover transitions on dose pills use a springy curve (`cubic-bezier(0.175, 0.885, 0.32, 1.275)`). Bouncy hover animations feel playful/tacky, undermining clinical confidence in a medical prescription context.
- **Fix**: Replace the springy easing with a smooth exponential ease-out curve like `cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1)` (ease-out-expo).
- **Suggested command**: `/impeccable animate`

### [P2] Visual Density of Safety Flags
- **Why it matters**: Safety Flags are presented as a high-density, flat block of checkboxes. When scanning quickly, it is easy for clinicians to miss important flags or experience cognitive fatigue.
- **Fix**: Segment the Safety Flags into distinct visually grouped rows (e.g. "Contraindications" vs "Interaction Flags") with subtle separators.
- **Suggested command**: `/impeccable layout`

### [P2] Readability of Half-Pills
- **Why it matters**: Half-pills use a white block overlay on the right half, which reduces contrast against white text inside the pill, making it hard for low-vision patients to read the dose number on the right side.
- **Fix**: Use a semi-transparent darker overlay or a clear split line instead of a solid white background block.
- **Suggested command**: `/impeccable typeset`

## Persona Red Flags

- **Alex (Power User / Clinician)**: Disrupted by native browser alert dialogs (e.g. `alert("คัดลอกลิงก์เรียบร้อยแล้ว")`) when copying patient URLs. These modal blocks require clicking "OK" or pressing Enter, which breaks their high-speed keyboard workflow.
- **Jordan (First-Timer)**: The W-code (`W120-2H`) is displayed prominently, but lacks an inline caption or tooltip explaining its format, forcing recall or confusion for new clinical users.
- **Sam (Accessibility-Dependent / Patient)**: Sam will struggle to distinguish half-pill values due to the white overlay washing out the text contrast. Additionally, Sam needs distinct pill outlines or sizes to tell standard pills apart without relying strictly on colors.

## Minor Observations
- Input fields do not have autofill attributes, which is fine for clinician-entered raw clinical values, but can trigger browser warnings.
- The modal layout uses a full-screen blurred overlay, which looks great but lacks focus-trapping for screen readers.

## Questions to Consider
- Can we replace native alerts with non-blocking toast notifications to preserve Alt-keyboard speed?
- Should we represent the Warfarin pill strengths (2 mg and 3 mg) with physical illustrations that resemble their true round shapes and scores?
